DESIGN
Background

DNA Origami
“DNA is the polymer that scientists dream about” [1]. Besides containing all human genetic information, its physical properties make it perfect to build nanoscale structures due to the following: 1. Watson-Crick base pairing complementarity 2. Ladder of antiparallel strands 3. Right-handed twist for B-DNA These three features allowed the discovery of DNA nanotechnology; construction of two and three dimensional shapes using DNA as a building material. Throughout the years, distinct branches of this field have been developed including DNA Origami [2]. Building beautiful, complex and functional structures by folding a piece of paper is much more transcendental than the macroscale art we all know as origami. In 2006, Paul Rothemund discovered a way in which DNA, the molecule of life, could be adapted into the Japanese technique of origami. The culmination of this discovery made scientists acknowledge that DNA Origami is one of the most powerful forms of DNA nanotechnology. The technique consists of folding a long strand of ssDNA (scaffold) using small oligonucleotides (staples) complementary to specific sections of the scaffold; achieving the construction of two and three dimensional structures [3] that can be potentially used in the areas of nanomedicine, physics, supramolecular chemistry and others.
Strand Displacement
Strand displacement is a mechanism applied in the field of DNA Origami that allows DNA structures to be more dynamic and versatile. Also known as “toehold-mediated strand displacement”, the mechanism consists of replacing one strand in a double helix with another one, the resulting double helix will be more stable and favorable in terms of free energy than the initial one.
DNA-RNA Hybrids
In human biology, RNA plays a vital role as a molecule of life. Part of this is due to its structure and chemical properties. Yet, it is also because its complexity that molecular RNA de novo design and engineering is limited [4]. As a solution, folding RNA has been proposed as an adaptation of the DNA origami technique. DNA-RNA hybridization attempts to give DNA nanotechnology a new field of application and functionality. The folding process of the RNA follows the same principles of DNA origami [5]; the complementarity between two strands, in this case, RNA and DNA. And similarly, RNA-DNA origami relies on protocols already used for nucleic-acid self-assembly. Distinct works have been developed in this area. There have been studies of the possible methodologies to hybridize these two molecules into complex non-functional nanostructures [4]. It has also been applied to construct RNA-DNA as siRNA delivery [6].
mRNA Therapy
what is mRNA?
To answer this question, it is important to define what a protein is. A protein is a “workhorse” of the cell [7], they carry out vital functions of life; enzymes, hormones, antibodies, they are all proteins. Proteins are expressed by genes; which are specific sections of DNA. The manufacture from DNA to proteins happens in two main steps: transcription and translation. In the first step, a DNA sequence is transcribed into messenger RNA (mRNA). In the second step, mRNA is translated into the amino acids that build up a protein [8]. Fundamentally, messenger RNA is a set of instructions to express any protein inside the human body, or more generally, inside any cell.
what is mRNA?
what is mRNA?
  1. Any protein can be encoded by mRNA
    • Enabling the development of prophylactic and therapeutic vaccines fighting diseases as diverse infections and cancer as well as protein replacement therapies.
  2. Follows native biology [11].
    • In order to express any protein or protein complexes, only natural cell processes are followed.
  3. mRNA does not interact with the genome [12], yet it harbors the exact elements required to express any encoded protein.
    • It does not enter the nucleus of a cell, therefore, detrimental integration with the genome is excluded.
    • By being non-replicative and metabolically decaying within a few days, it is merely a transient carrier of information.
  4. Comprises important pharmacologic characteristics.
    • The quantity supplied into the body can be controlled by doses.
    • Strong and potent.
    • Low cost manufacture [13].
Design
The problem
Our project is based on the idea the mRNA needs to be stabilized inside the human body in order to be delivered as a drug. By stabilizing mRNA we believe that mRNA half-life and translation levels can be increased, leading to the overall production of significant levels of the encoded protein over a longer timeframe [14]. Naked mRNA is quickly degraded by extracellular RNases and is not internalized efficiently [13]. Therefore, a great variety of in vitro and in vivo transfection reagents have been developed that facilitate cellular uptake of mRNA. These reagents can be divided into three main categories: physical methods (including electroporation and sonication); viral-based approaches, and nonviral vectors ( such as lipid-based delivery systems) [15]. mRNA based therapies are not the only RNA-related treatments that are being developed worldwide; small interfering RNA therapies are used in order to block protein expression [14]. The small size of siRNA allow it to be more easily protected against degradation than mRNA. Because its size, molecular weight, stability, and tendency to form secondary structures, mRNA delivery presents a more challenging endeavor. Our solution is to build a structure that stabilizes the mRNA molecule inside the human body. And what better way to do it than through a complementarity system?

NsrR: BBa_K2761005
This basic part consists of an optimized NsrR protein gene, which acts as a repressor of PyeaR promoter and is inhibited by the nitrate ion. E. coli natively produces a certain amount of NsrR. As such, the overexpression of NsrR can reduce any basal expression of GFP expressed by the PyeaR construct BBa_K381001 that may not be silenced by native NsrR. With a PyeaR reporter construct, the NsrR can be used to determine the presence of nitrate ion without any leaking.

msr-msd Scaffold: BBa_K2761008
Sequence for msr-msd transcript from EC86 retron, with specific PAM sequence for Cas1-Cas2 recognition.The msd-msr is transcribed as a single RNA sequence and interacts with the Retrotranscriptase (RT) EC86 protein to form an RNA-DNA chimera. The msr serves as a primer for the RT to start the retrotranscription, and the msd is the part of the transcript that is converted back into DNA. The msd is composed of a conserved part and a variable part. The conserved part is needed for retrotranscription and retron stability, while the variable part may be changed for any sequence of interest. In this part, the variable part of the msd contains an improved sequence that have high integration rates in the CRISPR array by adaptation proteins Cas1 and Cas2.

Improved Part:Nitrate inducible promoter: BBa_K2761011
The composite part BBa_K2761011 contains the NsrR gen regulated by a constitutive promoter. This segment was ligated to BBa_K381001 which contains a Pyear promoter and a GFP reporter gen. Pyear is inhibit by the presence of NsrR which is natively produced by the bacteria. Under this condition no fluorescence is expected. This characterized part is an improvement of a previous part for Gold requirements. The characterization of this part can be found in the Demonstrate segment.


Goals

E-coding design a set of composite parts that enable the recording of stimuli from environment and culture conditions in the bacterial genome. The SCRIBE system together with the CAS1-CAS2 complex enables the integration of any designed sequence in the CRISPR-array. The project expects that the following collection of parts can be used in any type of bacteria for molecular recording of events. In this case, all experimentation was base in strain .BL21 (DE3) Further experimentation in other types of strain is encouraged.
  1. Cas1-Cas2 cassette with wild type proteins for integration in the bacteria CRISPR array
  2. Cas1-Cas2 cassette with tag proteins, Flag and HA respectively, for rapid identification of protein production and activity of integration.
  3. RT-His cassette for the production of a retrotranscriptase with a histidine tag. Cassette involves for the production of the desired sequence for integration of the genome and analog for the stimulus in measurement.
  4. Ns production. This cassette is used primarily to sense the presence of nitrates in the environment.
